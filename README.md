#### Summaries - for those short on time 

**5-seconds**
- peer-to-peer, AI-human computer networks for unlocking human potential

**15 seconds**
- open source personal AI agents and networks can probably can unlock a lot of personal and collaborative potential, but a lot of hazards and asymmetries related to its use need to be considered and mitigated (cybersecurity risks, AI psychosis, addiction, poor attribution / anti-plagiarism mechanisms, lack of public participation, etc.)
- Peer-to-peer, bottom-up, AI-human computer networks, connected to other such networks, created and managed by users, could offer a way for a more democratized, humanist and less-asymmetrical AI and tech-startup ecosystem

**30-90 seconds**
- psychologically, we all probably operate from a "personal universe" in our minds, within a larger "multiverse of personal universes", interacting and reacting against each other in complex ways
- we've built complex systems to manage this complexity quite well (language, law, economies, societies, etc.) but we're building a new complex system (AI industry) that itself is crashing into other complex systems (socioeconomic, tech, biotech, cybersec, etc.), potentially opening up increased possibilities of Black Swan events.
- the general consensus is that solutions to this "meta-complexity" will come from top-down, centralized approaches (perhaps using AI or anti-AI measures), which has been well documented and discussed already.
- I'm personally interested in under-explored alternatives: bottom-up solutions from almost anyone, anywhere, to unlock untapped collaborative human potential.
- this could be done with an interesting mix of ingredients, that can be remixed in a number of ways:
	- personal AI agents with personal development, knowledge management, and education tools (representing "personal universes");
	- working peer-to-peer in experimental networks (communities, small networks);
	- with a dynamic attribution, virtue-ethics, shared and private knowledge management, incentives and game theory system (protocols);
	- to map out complex realities, asymmetries and possibilities (a complexity theory approach)
	- that scale to levels of complexity after an evolutionary process (experimental, informational, virtue-based, economic), with little to no downside for failure at each stage (evolutionary, complex adaptive systems);
	- and can merge, de-merge, fork, or use "genetic" snapshots of a previous network's blueprint at a particular stage;
	- to have ever-evolving small networks that collaborate with other networks, ensuring they never over-centralize and collapse under their own weight;
	- in combination - you have an ecosystem of "multiverses" that collaborate on "voting" on or building toward systems that work for them;
- with this kind of complex ecosystem, if safety measures and incentive mechanisms are well-engineered on each scale, you have the benefit of upside emerging (similar to a tech ecosystem where valuable startups emerge), while mitigating catastrophic downside (antifragility), and also allow users to participate in the overall design of the protocols.
- this "complex ecosystem" I label as "Acadine Network" - an ecosystem that contains "network" of networks, or "protocol" of protocols. Those sub-systems are defined by users, in an experimental, bottom-up fashion.

**Acadine Network (thought experiment, without analogies)**
In summary, the thought experiment I came up with, the "Acadine Network", is the following:
- A global interoperable ecosystem (perhaps simply just the internet) of open networks powered by  (perhaps "agentic networks" if we had to use a buzzword), each representing a particular worldview or shared "culture", working collaboratively to solve complex problems;
	- The networks essentially "reflect" the existing public data available on the internet, adding an extra layer of relational and "reputational" information networks, depending on different perspectives
- Each of these "agentic networks" are comprised of agentic computers, co-ordinating in a peer-to-peer fashion, via communication protocols and various game-theory incentives and disincentives;
	- They could operate as a particular specialized network for particular research, services or goals
- The networks themselves use a shared "network agent harness" as a protocol of rules between peers, channels, groups, other networks, users etc.; depending on the agreed-upon network architecture.
- Each peer could run a separate machine that they could connect their existing agent tools to; to make it fully interoperable with existing devices, UI, services
- These networks would have gone through an evolutionary stage of: 
	- **Personal** (individual peer trying out agentic AI with various tools of their own)
	- **Experimental** (a few peers in a small community tinkering with exchanging data, interactions, networks, ideas, and probably breaking a lot of things at this stage)
	- **Informational** (after achieving some degree of stability and verifiable utility - networks achieve a kind of distributed intelligence for some topic/goal)
		- Information stored in a public knowledge-graph (digital Zettelkasten)
	- **Virtuous** (use of virtue ethics, and layers of incentives, probabilistic and deterministic algorithms to formulate a "not-so-serious" virtue/skill scoring system for peers, attributed data, and even outside-of-network participants - in the sense that it will constantly evolve over time, so to not get gamed)
		- the utility of a "virtue" system allows for a much more dynamic kind of "reputation" system with n-dimensional axis, instead of "upvote/downvote"
		- we certainly don't want some dystopian "social credit score" system, so it's best to think of this system like an evolving MMORPG character scoring system - heuristics that are subject to being appealed, re-calculated, updated, forked, or completely scrapped if it causes problems
		- Proof-of-Virtue
	- **Economic** (after much experimentation, trial and error, and failing-while-the-stakes-are-low, a network could finally reach a stage roughly resembling a decentralized tech ecosystem)
		- startups and services as a distributed network
		- democratization: policies around value distribution: decentralized UBI
		- unlocking human potential: educational and career path tools: systems that help an individual identify and lining up they're best suited for, enjoy, and what the world really needs
	- **Exponential**
		- In the unlikely event that Artificial "Super" Intelligence emerges from a network (rapidly, self-improving AI), at this point, there would be multiple layers of fail-safes and no-single-point-of-failure in the system, unlike with centralized systems
- Each stage of these networks can be subject to: forks, de-merging, de-centralizing - to avoid a top-down, fragile system that collapse under their own weight.

#### Fundamental questions that lead to the idea of the Acadine Genesis Network

###### A fundamental question that guides the ideas:
- What would the world need to look like for every individual to have a "good life"?
	- let's say "good life" refers to typical western high standard of freedom, prosperity and well-being.
- How would you begin to answer that question?
	- There are various good answers
	- But, perhaps we don't have the appropriate language or systems to give a remarkable answer
		- What about a non-linear approach?
- Perhaps there are many bits of valuable fragmented answers scattered across various peoples, times, perspectives that, if combined in interesting ways, would yield better answers.
	- Each of us necessarily live and operate from an internal personal universe, comprised of experiences, biases, opinions on how we think the world is and should be, and how we predict or hope it will be in the future.
	- One proposed solution to co-ordinating such complexity is centralized Artificial Super Intelligence - but that comes with many fragilities.
	- A possible alternative is a less centralized system of networked agentic-computers

Therefore, one of the ways to start thinking about the "Acadine Network" as:
- an abstract concept, as a complex system of networks, for different ideas and perspectives across time; 
- a complex system to work with a multiverse of personal universes, and unlock collaborative value;

In broad terms, it would be something that could lead to what the original vision for the internet was:
- an ecosystem of networks that overlays on existing technology and information systems, necessary for it to exist;
- an open-source interface for a broad range of existing or future human-computer interfaces;
- leveraging AI+human capabilites through the network to augment existing information systems;
- open-source "worlds" (networks) of accessible and free (or affordable) ideas, research, reference material, and collaborators;
- variety of tools to help individuals and communities reach their full potential (AI + education technologies);
- A pinch of the right incentives, and other complex-systems ingredients, to make it all work together;
- ability to easily start / restart experimental networks from previous networks' "genetic" information in the form of "seeds" (snapshots, with anchors to the Bitcoin blockchain as a "trust anchor")

#### The general Problem: being alone at the center of our own universes

###### The multiverse of personal universes
- Each of us live and operate from an internal personal universe, comprised of experiences, biases, opinions on how we think the world is and should be, and how we predict or hope it will be in the future. We generally project this internal worldview onto others and all our interactions.
- we constantly clash our personal universes against reality, but mostly against each other. We also clash against the complex, wider "multiverse of personal universes", comprised of different combinations of personal and collective worldviews (teams, groups, communities, organizations, etc.).
- many collective or individual worldviews may have varying levels of validity and practical value. In fact, they must be differentiated, perhaps even opinionated, in order to be able to provide a unique contribution to the wider "multiverse" of worldviews.
- but, it's expensive, high-friction and time-consuming to translate our universes between each other, even with just one other partner. We sometimes find it hard to even keep track of our own personal universe, including our learnings, goals and plans. 
- and it's much harder to do all the above when trying to discuss or work on high-stakes, cross-disciplinary challenges that typically require hyper-specialization to even begin to enter the conversation (a clear example of this is the character of most debates/discussions around AI.
- to function in the real world, we typically default to heuristically shutting out competing worldviews.
- while we know valuable collaborative potential exists in the "multiverse" of complex mixtures of ideas, but we content ourselves to working within trusted, smaller local galaxies of shared values, such as in startups, communities, or institutions - with the occasional inter-galactic pings with "alien" intelligences, hoping to avoid a hostile response.
- this nudges aspiring individuals or small groups of specialists to a valid conclusion: it's psychologically and economically easier to conform to a smaller incremental goal within one's own field, than to think about collaborating on a larger scale.
###### Centralization and sub-optimal conformity
 - To navigate the complexity of the multiverse of worldviews, most of us typically settle for the default systems, institutions and incentives we inherit from our environment.
- in the context of technology, this has resulted in the centralized tech ecosystems that we have today (big tech, traditional VCs, universities, social media, etc.)
- the problem with these ecosystems, despite providing net economic (and perhaps social) gains to the world, they also tend to nudge our collective behaviors toward sub-optimal conformity
	-  the clearest example is how we are effectively forced to conform to social media "algorithms" 
	- and perhaps this leads toward drowning out signal with noise
- but the primary issue with a centralized tech ecosystem is the asymmetries it creates
- the clearest asymmetries can be seen from the AI industry, and development of AI itself: 
	- who had access to the knowledge and resources to develop it;
	- how data is collected and used in its training;
	- who has access to compute and the latest capabilities;
	- who can leverage it most effectively, to maintain an asymmetrical, anti-competitive advantage;
	- and who can dictate policies around its future development, safety and economic implications.
- this is not a personal attack on any particular company or persons; the situation is the result of settling for sub-optimal systems and incentives which force market participants to create asymmetries in order to even exist. 
	- this is also not a criticism of free market capitalism (which the centralized tech ecosystem does not conform to, in any case). 
	- rather, this is an open question of if there is a way to bring a decentralized counter-balance to the centralized tech ecosystems, and the AI industry in particular
- a simple (insufficient) analogy would be: "assuming what Bitcoin is to central banking, what would the decentralized network of tech and AI ecosystems look like?"
	- side-note: many decentralization and/or Bitcoin enthusiasts have thought about this for decades (and perhaps they have a sufficient answer already), but I wanted to further explore the ideas in the context of the popular rise of personal AI agents.
- so, ironically, AI, in the form of open-source coding and autonomous agents, may have opened a window of opportunity to explore the possibility of a decentralized AI ecosystem.
###### The window of opportunity for a decentralized AI ecosystem
- personalized AI agents, and AI in general, seems like it could provide an alternative to closed ecosystems, including the AI ecosystem itself.
- they can offer a way to begin to bridge the communication gap 
	- between the expert and non-expert (outside of social media, of course);
	- the humanities and engineering; 
	- the specialist and the generalist; 
	- between various valid worldviews; 
	- and unlock untapped collaborative potential between peers of individuals + their personal AI agents, within various kinds of communities or networks.
- something like this can already be done between trusted peers, in experimental smaller closed ecosystems
	- this could already be valuable to cross-disciplinary research between hyper-specialized fields
- could it also be done in a broader, more decentralized, more open and less-trusted environment?
- could such a network, if at all possible, unlock new kinds of value and collaboration? If so, it could democratize access and participation in the development of complex technology ecosystems, to act as a counter-balancing force to traditional, centralized tech ecosystems.
- the main idea of the Acadine Network is to push the bounds on how open and ubiquitous such a network could be, without collapsing under its own weight, causing harm, or self-defeating its founding purpose by becoming another over-centralized system.
###### A protocol of protocols for a multiverse of personal universes
- Communication protocols determine much of our interconnected digital lives
- engineers know that the average user will never touch or even be aware of such protocols (such as those in the OSI model)
- rarely is a new protocol needed; usually created for a new experimental network (such as was the case in Bitcoin)
- personalized AI agents offer a potential opportunity for new kinds of experimental networks of peers
	- to do the heavy lifting of connecting parts of our personal universes we want to share with the multiverse of ideas, outlooks, and predictions about the future;
	- and to optimize our best collaborative potential for the most valuable work we can do;
- personalized AI agents also offer an opportunity for the average user or community to have a say in the development of advanced technologies
	- AI is driving down the cost of having a technical opinion for one's personal use in the technology
	- for the first time, the average user can have a reasonably clear understanding of the implications of the technology they're using
	- while it's not enough yet to provide an *informed* technical opinion in the development of the technology, it's a starting point toward self-education and being able to make choices between a multiplicity of AI models, tools, products, communities and even networks and protocols.
- therefore, the most consequential use-case I can personally think of for personalized AI agents: is an adaptable framework of protocols for a widely accessible, peer-to-peer intelligence network that's augmented by both humans and AI, for new kinds of collaborative technology ecosystems, that can co-ordinate across complex systems to solve the most complex challenges 
	- to achieve such an idealized global protocol, it would be comprised of a multiplicity of "sub-networks" of various sizes and scales, interacting symmetrically according to a complex co-ordination of democratized protocols
	- such a protocol would reflect a multiplicity of worldviews, that cohesively contributes to a future that's democratically determined
- I call this the Acadine Network. It's speculative science-fiction at this point, and may remain so for a long-time
	- but in the meantime, it can be a guiding principle for smaller, adaptable, experimental networks
###### The Acadine Network
- generally, it could be something like a protocol stack for dynamic networks of peer-to-peer, democratic, humanitarian AI and collaborative technology ecosystems.
- I am confident this is not a new idea - I've tried to keep it as broad as possible, to provide a personal north star for myself, future collaborators, overlapping/adjacent projects, or just providing inspiration for something entirely different
- I believe something like Acadine Network is something will ultimately need to be determined by a network of smaller P2P networks, or a 
- but broadly speaking; the network should probably achieve something like symmetrical, democratic access to AI and technological policies, decision-making, research, development, value creation and distribution, to solve for generally beneficial exponential technological progress combined with existential risk prevention.

This may seem overwhelmingly complex and unnecessary. What would be the payoffs, even if the above is remotely feasible?
Here's an oversimplified example of co-ordinating research, development and safety efforts between two hyper-specialized fields across multiple, unaligned organizations, during an emerging breakthrough:
- a biotech research lab is on the brink of a fundamental breakthrough in cellular/molecular biology that could save lives;
- this breakthrough would open a small window of opportunity of vulnerability in the safety systems of a digital biology lab, who's new development is also about to be rapidly released in order to save lives;
- both parties were not intending harm, and did their best efforts to prevent harm in their own systems, but a complex combination of developments released in a short window would have unforseen consequences;
- Acadine Network, in its idealized state, would have early-warning systems to provide pre-emptive, co-ordinated R&D-plus-safety efforts, for both specialized fields (without necessarily delaying their breakthroughs), to prevent a biotech catastrophe.

The above example illustrates a simple co-ordination problem. With potentially multiple AI and R&D breakthroughs happening in parallel, there would need to be proactive, co-ordinated, multidisciplinary risk-mitgation efforts.

While a simpler solution would be to simply to slow down the acceleration, that is probably less feasible for a variety of reasons, starting from a simple game-theoretic perspective.

The technical, security, economic and safety challenges involved to even begin to work toward such a network would require a complex multidisciplinary approach, which is the subject of the next section. But, this is going be my personal-universe version of a starting point for the network. There may be hundreds, thousands, or millions of versions of something like the Acadine Network, born from overlaying different worldviews, moral frameworks, or technical implementations. The point is to have a starting point.

###### Starting Acadine Network from within one's own personal universe (worldview)
Acadine Genesis Network, my version, is my starting point. Everyone will have their own version, based on their personal universe, and they would perhaps converge toward with their nearest adjacent and overlapping neighbors.

##### Archetype of the Fool
Foolishness might seem to be a strange topic to introduce when one's end goal is some kind of viable technical proposal. But in the context of trying something new, the fool is a perfect archetype to follow and deeply consider. The archetypal fool in fables and history typically takes the form of: 
- an unlikely hero, stumbling into a journey of sorts, perhaps with friends (starting something new)
- a jester, who's role it is to poke fun at the ruling class (not taking oneself too seriously)

The fool was the only member of medieval court that could reflect back the foolishness of the king. Wise courts would welcome this, for the fool represents renewal, creativity and the ability to shed a psychological rigidity. Only an insane king would strike down a fool, for that demonstrates a weakness that could soon lead to dethroning. So, the fool has counter-intuitive features, that makes it one of the most under-rated archetypes.

In this way, the fool has a different kind of power. A fool is antifragile; possessing rare privileges, protections and freedoms - mostly to explore new ideas and creative expressions. But a fool has one critical limiting characteristic: a true fool has no aspiration to be a king. This is part of a fool's appeal, and hidden form of power. Everyone appreciates the archetype of the fool. It reflects some of the best of our human nature: innocence, sincerity, creativity, courage, intelligence, humor and wit.

In recent popular culture, the fool-archetype and its utility has become well-recognized. As such, technology leaders and governmental institutions have made poor attempts to act as a fool - particularly on social media. While on a surface-level, this can be somewhat entertaining, deep down we know its merely kings trying to emulate the popular appeal of the fool. This backfires incredibly on them, for a simple reason:

A king can never be a fool, and a fool can never be a king.

The relationship is asymmetrical but synergistic: a true fool has no aspiration to be a king, and a king can never aspire to be a fool. But, like in medieval courts, the king and fool can mutually support each other to some positive end. A virtuous fool, despite their unattractive social standing, indeed can change the course of a failing kingdom.

###### The virtuous fool
Acadine Genesis Network represents a virtuous kind of foolishness. If it remains true to its purpose, the network would never seek to be more than a decentralized community of like-minded "virtuous fools", trying interesting experiments to some positive end. This unlocks a reserve of counter-intuitive creative thinking, that results in further contrarian principles that may be effective in the context of P2P networks:

- **Not trying to raise funding:** most startups or researchers would perhaps want to rush to raise capital for an idea like this. Rather, it's probably more effective to demonstrate that anyone, anywhere, without funding, and a creative idea, can have more impact than yet another venture or non-profit. You could inspire a small community of highly-agile, distributed, fool-creators that are collaborating with other like-minded communities, in ways that are more effective than a highly-funded, talent-rich, but utterly rigid team. 

- **Not trying to scale:** conventional logic would dictate that you would need to scale to compete with incumbents, and act as a counter-balance to the asymmetries they're creating. But, most of the frontier AI labs are the result of this kind of thinking, because they're stuck in complex systems, economic and political, that forces them to centralize and create further asymmetries. Rather, what's probably needed is a simple biologically-inspired model applied to growing distributed P2P networks: cellular division, de-merging, atomization, and continual attempts at remaining decentralized in an ocean of interconnecting (or disconnecting) communities. In this sense, it creates an "anti-network-effect", where it doesn't force individuals to put up with suboptimal centralized networks because of their strong network effects.

- **Appealing to the humanities:** interestingly, the AI industry has altogether embraced the humanities (specifically, for their content for training data), but there has also been a hiring strategy involving philosophers, psychologists, sociologists and more. However, most of this employment of the humanities appears to be for practical economic purposes at best, or virtue-signaling at worst. Rather, I am considering a true bridging between the humanities and engineering for P2P protocols. Philosophies of Language, Mind, and Complex Systems (and many more), could be combined with their respective computational fields (NLP, computational neuroscience, systems engineering, etc.). But even less considered, is the need for a true consideration of the artists, which is the focus of the next point

- **Non-linear creative approaches to problem-solving:** in a complex system, and for complex problems in general, linear approaches to work and problem-solving might be insufficient or even counter-productive. The most counter-intuitive idea I've come across is the possibility that "non-work": recreation, art, spending time with family, even just cooking a meal - these aren't merely a means to recharge, so you can get back more effectively to work. They may end up being the very essential inspiration or non-linear path toward solving something existential, that wasn't even considered in one's frame of reference. If you ascribe to this kind of philosophical approach to complexity theory [3], then you can't predict in advance what is truly important to one's goals and meta-goals. This can be considered part of an antifragile approach to living, and is also an essential approach when considering the design of the Acadine Genesis Network. Art isn't a means to an end. In my mind, it's of equal importance as engineering is, to fulfil the potential for the network.

- **Virtue-based ethics:** instead of consequentialist or rules-based ethics.
